Ethical Analysis

Critical Discussion of ChatGPT

ChatGPT is useful, but its social impact depends on how people use it, how developers design it, and how institutions regulate it. The same tool can support learning and productivity, but it can also cause harm when used carelessly.

Ethical Lens 1: Utilitarianism

Utilitarianism asks whether ChatGPT creates the greatest good for the greatest number of people.

Positive View

ChatGPT can help many people save time, learn faster, improve accessibility, and complete work more efficiently.

Concern

If it causes misinformation, job loss, weaker learning, or unfair outcomes, then the overall benefit to society becomes less clear.

Ethical Lens 2: Deontology

Deontology focuses on duties, rules, honesty, and moral responsibility.

Positive View

ChatGPT can be ethical when users are honest about using it and when it supports learning, work, or communication responsibly.

Concern

Using ChatGPT to cheat, mislead others, spread false information, or avoid responsibility is unethical even if it saves time.

Major Social Implications

Misinformation

ChatGPT can sound confident even when it is wrong, which may cause people to trust false information.

Bias

AI may reflect unfair patterns from the data it was trained on, which can shape how users think and decide.

Privacy

Users may enter personal, school, work, or health information without understanding how sensitive it is.

Job Displacement

Some tasks in writing, customer service, and administrative work may be automated, affecting workers.

Learning Loss

Students may become dependent on instant answers and develop weaker problem-solving habits.

Accountability

When AI causes harm, it can be unclear whether the user, company, developer, or institution is responsible.

How the Ethical Lenses Change the Interpretation

Question Utilitarianism Deontology
Is ChatGPT good? It is good if its benefits outweigh its harms. It is good if it is used honestly and responsibly.
Is using it for school ethical? It depends on whether it improves learning overall. It is unethical if it breaks academic honesty rules.
Should healthcare use it? Yes, if it improves patient care and reduces workload. Only if professionals remain responsible and patients are protected.
